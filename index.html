Effect of Potassium Nitrate on Growth of Rapid Cycling Brassica rapa (Wisconsin Fast Plants) in the Presence of Glyphosate

Suvit Asnani, Dr. Dina Ellsworth

High Technology High School, 765 Newman Springs Rd, Lincroft, NJ 07738

Abstract

  • Glyphosate works by interrupting the shikimate pathway
  • This study aimed to see if potassium nitrate could reverse the effects of glyphosate in case of an accident
  • It was hypothesized that the nitrate content in a moderate amount of potassium nitrate would overcome the effects of glyphosate by allowing the shikimate pathway to function properly again in Rapid-cycling Brassica rapa
  • The study was conducted by growing 50 Rapid-cycling Brassica rapa and splitting them up into 5 groups of 10
  • There was one positive control, one negative control, and 3 experimental groups (low, medium, and high concentration solutions)
  • The length of the plants before and after one week of experimentation was recorded, along with the dry biomass
  • An ANOVA Statistical Test was run on the change in lengths, and T-tests were run on the data after the ANOVA came back statistically significant
  • The data supported the hypothesis, as the length of plants in medium and high concentrations of potassium nitrate was statistically significantly longer compared to the negative control
  • Further, the length of the plants in the high concentration had no statistically significant growth as compared to the positive control, showing that they were similar values
  • It was found that a moderate or high concentration of potassium nitrate may be able to reverse the effects of glyphosate

Background

Glyphosate is one of the most popular herbicides used worldwide and works by interrupting the shikimate pathway. Glyphosate inhibits an enzyme from working correctly in a plant, stopping production of essential amino acids and killing the plant.

Potassium nitrate is an effective fertilizer and contains important plant nutrients. Both potassium and nitrate have been found to be beneficial to plants in a moderate quanitity.

Finally, little is known about the effect of other introduced chemicals, specifically potassium nitrate, on the effect of glyphosate and its growth on plants.

Rationale

While being used, glyphosate can accidentally be introduced to crops. Some crops are engineered to be resistant to glyphosate, allowing farmers to spray their entire field without worrying about it affecting their crops. However, not all crops are genetically engineered. It is known that potassium nitrate has a beneficial effect on plants when used in moderate amounts. If a plant is accidentally introduced to glyphosate, it could die. If it was known that something, in this case, potassium nitrate, could overcome glyphotate, plants could be saved from dying. This study aimed to see if potassium nitrate could reverse the effects of glyphosate to save crops from dying in case of accidents.
